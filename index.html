    Not a medical device. The cry reading is a hint, and any fever guidance comes from the temperature you enter — not the sound. It can't diagnose illness. If your baby has a fever (especially if under 3 months), is breathing oddly, won't feed, is unusually limp or floppy, or stays inconsolable, contact a doctor or emergency services right away.

    How accurate is this, really? ›

    Honest answer: it's a helpful hint, not a verdict. Lullaby measures real acoustic features of the cry — fundamental pitch, brightness, loudness, and the burst‑and‑pause rhythm — and matches them against the documented sound signatures of common cry types (rhythmic = hungry, high‑pitched and continuous = discomfort, breathy and low = tired, and so on).

    Even the best research models trained on labelled cry datasets reach roughly 70–90% on clean recordings, and lower in a noisy home. So treat the percentage as a lean, not a fact. Adding a measured temperature helps because it covers what sound can't: the cry alone can't reveal fever, but a thermometer can — so the two together give a fuller picture and flag illness the audio would miss. You know your baby's cues and routine better than any model — use this alongside your judgement.
